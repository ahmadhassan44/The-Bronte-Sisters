Jane Eyre

by Charlotte Brontë • Published: 1847

A groundbreaking novel that explores the emotional and moral development of the fiercely independent Jane Eyre. The story weaves themes of love, social criticism, and feminism, making it a timeless classic.

Wuthering Heights

by Emily Brontë • Published: 1847

A haunting tale of passion, revenge, and the destructive power of love set against the bleak, windswept Yorkshire moors. Wuthering Heights explores intense emotions and complex relationships that transcend generations, making it a masterpiece of Gothic fiction.

The Tenant of Wildfell Hall

by Anne Brontë • Published: 1848

A powerful and disturbing tale of a woman's flight from an abusive marriage, challenging Victorian social conventions. The novel addresses themes of alcoholism, gender equality, and the rights of women, making it one of the first feminist novels.

Villette

by Charlotte Brontë • Published: 1853

Following the story of Lucy Snowe, who travels to the fictional French-speaking city of Villette to teach at a girls' school, the novel explores themes of isolation, repression, and the struggle for independence.

Agnes Grey

by Anne Brontë • Published: 1847

Drawing from Anne's own experiences as a governess, this novel provides a realistic portrayal of the life of a Victorian governess, exposing the difficulties and humiliations they often faced.

Shirley

by Charlotte Brontë • Published: 1849

Set during the Luddite uprisings in Yorkshire, this social novel interweaves themes of romance with political and social commentary. Through its heroines Shirley and Caroline, the novel explores women's roles in society and questions of industrial reform.

★ ★ ★ ★ ★

The Professor

by Charlotte Brontë • Published: 1857

Charlotte Brontë's first novel, published posthumously, follows William Crimsworth as he becomes a professor in an all-girls school. The novel draws on her experiences in Brussels and explores themes of love, independence, and social class.
